\begin{abstract}
Accurate Estimated Time of Arrival (ETA) prediction for ocean-going vessels is critical for port scheduling, supply chain optimization, and maritime safety.
Existing approaches typically treat the problem as monolithic time-series regression, neglecting the distinct physical processes governing open-sea sailing and port operations.
We propose a decoupled dual-model architecture that separates sailing-time prediction from port-dwell-time estimation.
For the sailing component, we adapt the Informer model---which features ProbSparse self-attention with $O(L \log L)$ complexity---to process 11-dimensional AIS and meteorological features over 48-step input sequences.
An asymmetric Huber loss with voyage-length weighting is introduced to penalize underestimation of long-haul voyages, improving operational reliability at a modest cost to symmetric error metrics.
The port-dwell component employs a lightweight MLP with region-aware encoding.
Experiments on a large-scale dataset of 35.9 million AIS records spanning 9,087 trans-Pacific voyages demonstrate that the proposed method achieves competitive performance (MAE of 16.20 hours) while significantly reducing late-arrival predictions.
Ablation studies confirm that incorporating weather features reduces MAE by 7.1\% compared to navigation-only inputs, benefiting all model architectures.
\end{abstract}

ETA prediction, Informer, AIS data, weather features, attention mechanism, maritime transportation

\section{Introduction}

Maritime transportation accounts for approximately 80\% of global trade by volume~\cite{unctad2023}.
Accurate vessel Estimated Time of Arrival (ETA) prediction is essential for port resource allocation, berth planning, and supply chain coordination.
Even modest improvements in ETA accuracy can yield significant economic benefits by reducing vessel idle time and optimizing terminal operations~\cite{park2021}.

Traditional ETA estimation relies on ship-reported ETAs broadcast via the Automatic Identification System (AIS), which are often inaccurate due to manual entry, outdated routing plans, and unforeseen weather conditions~\cite{fang2022}.
Data-driven approaches have emerged as a promising alternative, leveraging historical AIS trajectories to learn voyage patterns.
Early methods employed linear regression~\cite{zhang2019rf} and support vector regression~\cite{liu2019svr}, while more recent work has adopted deep learning architectures including LSTMs~\cite{zhang2022lstm}, GRUs~\cite{han2021gru}, and Transformer variants~\cite{dong2023transformer}.

However, existing methods face three key limitations:
(1) Standard recurrent models struggle with long-sequence dependencies inherent in trans-oceanic voyages that may span weeks;
(2) most approaches treat the entire arrival process monolithically, conflating open-sea sailing dynamics with port-specific dwell behaviors;
and (3) the influence of en-route meteorological conditions on travel time is seldom incorporated.

The Informer architecture~\cite{zhou2021informer} was proposed specifically for long-sequence time-series forecasting (LSTF), introducing ProbSparse self-attention that reduces computational complexity from $O(L^2)$ to $O(L \log L)$ while maintaining prediction accuracy.
Recent work has demonstrated its effectiveness for ship trajectory prediction~\cite{xiong2024informertp}, motivating its application to the ETA estimation problem.

In this paper, we make the following contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item We propose a \textbf{decoupled dual-model architecture} that separates sailing-time prediction (Informer) from port-dwell-time estimation (MLP), reflecting the distinct physical processes underlying each component.
    \item We design an \textbf{asymmetric Huber loss} with voyage-length weighting to address the systematic underestimation of long-haul voyages.
    \item We incorporate \textbf{five meteorological features} (temperature, wind speed, wind level, atmospheric pressure, and visibility) alongside six navigation features, demonstrating measurable improvements through ablation analysis.
    \item We conduct extensive experiments on a \textbf{large-scale trans-Pacific dataset} comprising 35.9 million AIS records from 3,940 vessels, comparing against five baseline methods.
\end{enumerate}


%==============================================================
\section{Methodology}
%==============================================================

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

Given a vessel's historical AIS trajectory $\mathbf{X} = \{x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_T\}$, where each observation $x_t \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$ contains $d$-dimensional navigation and meteorological features at time step $t$, the objective is to predict the remaining sailing time $\hat{y} \in \mathbb{R}^{+}$ until the vessel reaches its destination port.

We decompose the total ETA into two components:
\begin{equation}
    \text{ETA}_{\text{total}} = \hat{y}_{\text{sail}} + \hat{y}_{\text{port}}
    \label{eq:decomposition}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{y}_{\text{sail}}$ denotes the predicted open-sea sailing time and $\hat{y}_{\text{port}}$ denotes the predicted port-dwell time.
This decomposition is motivated by the observation that sailing dynamics (governed by vessel speed, currents, and weather) and port operations (governed by berth availability, cargo handling, and congestion) are driven by fundamentally different factors.

\subsection{System Architecture}

The proposed system consists of three stages, as illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:architecture}:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Data Preprocessing}: Raw AIS records are cleaned, segmented into individual voyages, augmented with meteorological features, and organized into fixed-length input sequences.
    \item \textbf{Sailing Time Prediction}: An Informer-based encoder-decoder model processes the input sequences to predict the remaining sailing time.
    \item \textbf{Port Dwell Prediction}: A separate MLP model estimates the expected port-dwell time based on destination region, arrival timing, and location features.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Input Representation}

Each input sequence consists of $L_{\text{enc}} = 48$ consecutive AIS observations.
At each time step $t$, the feature vector $x_t \in \mathbb{R}^{11}$ comprises:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Navigation features} ($d_{\text{nav}} = 6$): latitude, longitude, speed over ground (SOG), course over ground (COG), great-circle distance to destination ($\text{dist\_km}$), and bearing difference ($\Delta\theta$) between COG and the bearing toward the destination.
    \item \textbf{Meteorological features} ($d_{\text{wx}} = 5$): temperature, wind speed, wind level, mean sea-level pressure (PRMSL), and visibility.
\end{itemize}

The distance to destination and bearing difference are computed using the Haversine formula:
\begin{equation}
    d = 2R \arcsin\!\left(\sqrt{\sin^2\!\frac{\Delta\phi}{2} + \cos\phi_1 \cos\phi_2 \sin^2\!\frac{\Delta\lambda}{2}}\right)
    \label{eq:haversine}
\end{equation}
where $R = 6{,}371$ km is the Earth's radius, $\phi$ denotes latitude, and $\lambda$ denotes longitude.

Navigation features undergo min-max normalization to $[0, 1]$ with clipping at $[-0.5, 1.5]$ to handle out-of-distribution test samples.
The target variable (remaining hours) is transformed via log-standardization: $\tilde{y} = (\ln(1+y) - \mu) / \sigma$, where $\mu$ and $\sigma$ are computed from the training set.

In addition to the raw features, the model receives temporal embeddings for each time step, encoding minute, hour, weekday, day-of-month, and month as continuous values normalized to $[-0.5, 0.5]$.
These temporal markers enable the model to capture periodic patterns such as diurnal traffic variations and seasonal weather effects.


\subsection{Informer-Based Sailing Time Predictor}

\subsubsection{Architecture Overview}

We adopt the Informer architecture~\cite{zhou2021informer} for the sailing time prediction component due to its three key advantages over recurrent alternatives:
(1) ProbSparse self-attention reduces the quadratic complexity of standard Transformers to $O(L \log L)$, enabling efficient processing of long input sequences;
(2) the self-attention distilling operation progressively reduces sequence length through the encoder, creating a multi-scale feature hierarchy;
and (3) the generative-style decoder produces predictions in a single forward pass rather than autoregressively, avoiding error accumulation.

The model configuration is summarized in Table~\ref{tab:model_config}.
The encoder consists of $E = 2$ layers with ProbSparse self-attention, interleaved with distilling layers (Conv1d + MaxPool) that halve the sequence length at each level.
The decoder uses $D = 1$ layer with masked ProbSparse self-attention followed by full cross-attention to the encoder output.

\subsubsection{Input Embedding}

Following the original Informer design~\cite{zhou2021informer}, the input embedding combines three components:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{X}_{\text{feed}}^{(t)} = \alpha \, \mathbf{u}_t + \text{PE}_{t} + \sum_{p} \text{SE}_{t}^{(p)}
    \label{eq:embedding}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{u}_t$ is the scalar projection of raw features through a 1D convolution (kernel size 3, circular padding) that maps $\mathbb{R}^{d} \to \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{model}}}$; $\text{PE}_{t}$ is fixed sinusoidal positional encoding; and $\text{SE}_{t}^{(p)}$ are learnable temporal embeddings for each time granularity $p \in \{\text{minute, hour, weekday, day, month}\}$, projected through a linear layer to $\mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{model}}}$.

\subsubsection{ProbSparse Self-Attention}

In standard self-attention, the attention matrix computation requires $O(L^2)$ time and memory.
The ProbSparse mechanism~\cite{zhou2021informer} addresses this by identifying the most informative queries through a KL-divergence-based sparsity measure:
\begin{equation}
    M(q_i, K) = \max_j \frac{q_i k_j^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}} - \frac{1}{L_K} \sum_{j=1}^{L_K} \frac{q_i k_j^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}
    \label{eq:probsparse}
\end{equation}
where $q_i$ and $k_j$ are query and key vectors, respectively.
Only the top-$u$ queries (where $u = c \cdot \ln L_K$, $c = 5$) with the highest $M$ values receive computed attention; the remaining queries are assigned the mean value of $V$.

\subsubsection{Encoder Distilling}

Between successive encoder layers, a distilling operation reduces the sequence length:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{X}_{j+1}^t = \text{MaxPool}\!\left(\text{ELU}\!\left(\text{Conv1d}\!\left([\mathbf{X}_j^t]_{\text{AB}}\right)\right)\right)
    \label{eq:distilling}
\end{equation}
where $[\cdot]_{\text{AB}}$ denotes the output of the attention block.
This creates a pyramidal structure that captures features at multiple temporal scales---a desirable property for ETA prediction where both recent maneuvers and long-term routing patterns are informative.

\subsubsection{Decoder and Output}

The decoder receives a start token consisting of the last $L_{\text{label}} = 24$ steps of the encoder input, concatenated with $L_{\text{pred}} = 1$ zero-padded placeholder positions.
It applies masked ProbSparse self-attention followed by full cross-attention with the encoder output.
A final linear projection maps from $d_{\text{model}}$ to $c_{\text{out}} = 1$, producing the predicted remaining time in normalized log-space.

The prediction is denormalized as:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{y} = \exp(\tilde{y} \cdot \sigma + \mu) - 1
    \label{eq:denorm}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Asymmetric Huber Loss}

Standard loss functions (MSE, MAE, Huber) treat overestimation and underestimation symmetrically.
In maritime logistics, however, underestimating the arrival time is often more costly than overestimating it, as it can lead to missed berth windows and cascading scheduling failures.
Furthermore, prediction errors on long-haul voyages have greater operational impact than errors on short voyages.

We propose an \textit{Asymmetric Huber Loss} that addresses both concerns:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} w_{\text{asym}}^{(i)} \cdot w_{\text{target}}^{(i)} \cdot \mathcal{H}_\delta(e_i)
    \label{eq:loss}
\end{equation}
where $e_i = \hat{y}_i - y_i$ is the prediction error, $\mathcal{H}_\delta$ is the standard Huber function with threshold $\delta = 1.0$:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{H}_\delta(e) = \begin{cases}
        \frac{1}{2} e^2 & \text{if } |e| \leq \delta \\
        \delta (|e| - \frac{1}{2}\delta) & \text{otherwise}
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:huber}
\end{equation}

The asymmetric weight penalizes underestimation by a factor of $\alpha = 1.5$:
\begin{equation}
    w_{\text{asym}}^{(i)} = \begin{cases}
        \alpha & \text{if } e_i < 0 \text{ (underestimation)} \\
        1 & \text{otherwise}
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:asym_weight}
\end{equation}

The target weight increases the loss contribution for long-haul voyages:
\begin{equation}
    w_{\text{target}}^{(i)} = 1 + \beta \cdot \text{ReLU}(y_i)
    \label{eq:target_weight}
\end{equation}
where $\beta = 0.3$ controls the weighting strength and $y_i$ is the normalized target value (positive for above-average voyage durations).


\subsection{Port Dwell Time Predictor}

The port-dwell-time predictor is a two-layer MLP ($64 \to 32 \to 1$) with ReLU activation, batch normalization, and 20\% dropout.
It takes as input the destination port region (one-hot encoded among six predefined regions: China East Coast, Singapore, Middle East, Suez, U.S.\ West Coast, and Pacific), arrival coordinates, and cyclically encoded temporal features (month and weekday as sine/cosine pairs).

The model is trained separately on historical port-stop records extracted from AIS data, using a Huber loss on log-transformed dwell durations.
At inference time, each voyage's predicted sailing time is augmented by the predicted port-dwell time according to Eq.~\eqref{eq:decomposition}.

\section{Experiments}
%==============================================================

\subsection{Dataset}

We use a proprietary AIS dataset covering trans-Pacific shipping routes between Chinese ports and the U.S.\ West Coast, spanning from January 2021 to March 2025.
The raw dataset contains approximately 35.9 million AIS position reports from 3,940 unique vessels.

Each AIS record includes the Maritime Mobile Service Identity (MMSI), timestamp, longitude, latitude, speed over ground (SOG, in knots), and course over ground (COG, in degrees).
Meteorological observations---temperature, wind speed, wind level (Beaufort scale), mean sea-level pressure, and horizontal visibility---are matched to each AIS record from contemporary weather data sources.

\subsubsection{Voyage Extraction}

Individual voyages are extracted from the continuous AIS stream using a speed-based segmentation approach.
A vessel is classified as \textit{stopped} when its SOG falls below 0.3 knots for a sustained period.
Contiguous sailing segments with duration $\geq 6$ hours and $\geq 50$ AIS records are retained as valid voyages, yielding 9,087 voyages after filtering.

The remaining time to arrival for each AIS observation is computed as:
\begin{equation}
    y_t = t_{\text{end}} - t
\end{equation}
where $t_{\text{end}}$ is the timestamp of the last observation in the voyage (arrival).
Voyages exceeding 720 hours (30 days) are excluded as likely erroneous or multi-stop itineraries.

\subsubsection{Data Quality}

Several quality filters are applied:
records with missing navigation features are dropped;
weather features are forward-filled within each vessel's timeline and remaining gaps are zero-filled;
SOG values outside $[0, 30]$ knots and remaining times outside $[0, 720]$ hours are excluded;
and a minimum data density of 10 records per day is enforced per vessel.

After preprocessing, the dataset is partitioned by voyage into 70\% training, 15\% validation, and 15\% test splits, resulting in approximately 13.1 million training sequences, 2.9 million validation sequences, and 2.9 million test sequences.

\subsection{Preprocessing}

The preprocessing pipeline operates in three stages:

\paragraph{Stage 1: Per-vessel segmentation}
Raw CSV files (totaling 5.5 GB across five yearly files) are processed in parallel using 32 workers.
For each vessel identified by its MMSI, the AIS records are sorted chronologically.
Speed-based segmentation splits the timeline into sailing and stopped segments, with stops defined by $\text{SOG} < 0.3$ knots sustained for $\geq 2$ hours.

\paragraph{Stage 2: Feature engineering}
For each sailing segment, two geometric features are computed relative to the voyage endpoint:
the great-circle distance to the destination (Eq.~\eqref{eq:haversine}) and the bearing difference $\Delta\theta = |\theta_{\text{dest}} - \text{COG}|$ (wrapped to $[0^\circ, 180^\circ]$).
These features provide the model with explicit spatial context about the vessel's progress toward its destination.

\paragraph{Stage 3: Sequence construction}
Sliding windows of length $L_{\text{enc}} = 48$ are extracted from each voyage, with the target being the remaining hours at the window's final time step.
A stratified sampling strategy ensures adequate representation of long-haul voyages ($>400$ hours), which receive 3$\times$ the sampling weight of short voyages ($<100$ hours).
All features are normalized, and the decoder input is constructed by concatenating the last $L_{\text{label}} = 24$ encoder steps with $L_{\text{pred}} = 1$ zero-padded position.


\subsection{Training Configuration}

All models are trained on a single NVIDIA RTX PRO 6000 GPU (96 GB VRAM) with a batch size of 4,096.
The Informer model uses the Adam optimizer~\cite{kingma2015adam} with an initial learning rate of $4 \times 10^{-4}$ and a ReduceLROnPlateau scheduler (factor 0.5, patience 1).
Early stopping with patience 3 is applied based on validation loss.
The asymmetric Huber loss (Eq.~\eqref{eq:loss}) with $\delta = 1.0$, $\alpha = 1.5$, and $\beta = 0.3$ is used throughout.

Data loading employs memory-mapped arrays (NumPy memmap) to handle the 13M+ training sequences without exceeding system memory, with 64 data loader workers and pinned GPU memory transfers.


\subsection{Baseline Methods}

We compare against five baseline methods, all trained and evaluated on the identical dataset splits:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{LSTM}~\cite{hochreiter1997lstm}: A two-layer LSTM ($h = 256$) with the last hidden state projected to a scalar prediction. 802K parameters.
    \item \textbf{GRU}~\cite{cho2014gru}: A two-layer GRU ($h = 256$) with analogous architecture. 610K parameters.
    \item \textbf{MLP}: A three-layer feedforward network ($512 \to 256 \to 128$) operating on flattened 48$\times$11 input vectors, with batch normalization and dropout. 270K parameters.
    \item \textbf{XGBoost}~\cite{chen2016xgboost}: Gradient-boosted trees trained on hand-crafted statistical features (last-step, mean, standard deviation, and delta features across the 48-step window). 500 boosting rounds with GPU acceleration.
    \item \textbf{Ridge Regression}: $L_2$-regularized linear regression on last-step and mean features, serving as the simplest baseline.
\end{enumerate}

All neural baselines use the Huber loss ($\delta = 1.0$), Adam optimizer ($\text{lr} = 10^{-3}$), and early stopping with patience 3.


\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

We report the following metrics on the test set:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{MAE} (Mean Absolute Error, in hours): $\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i}|\hat{y}_i - y_i|$
    \item \textbf{RMSE} (Root Mean Squared Error, in hours): $\sqrt{\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i}(\hat{y}_i - y_i)^2}$
    \item \textbf{MAPE} (Mean Absolute Percentage Error, \%): computed only on samples where $y_i > 24$ hours, to avoid division-by-zero artifacts on near-arrival predictions.
\end{itemize}


\subsection{Main Results}

Table~\ref{tab:main_results} presents the comparison of all methods on the test set.
All models share the same input features, data splits, and normalization.
However, the Informer model is trained with the proposed asymmetric Huber loss (Eq.~\eqref{eq:loss}), whereas the baseline models use the standard Huber loss (Eq.~\eqref{eq:huber}).

\subsubsection{Symmetric Metric Comparison}

Among the baselines trained with standard Huber loss, XGBoost achieves the lowest MAE (15.14 hours) using hand-crafted statistical features (last-step, mean, standard deviation, and delta across the 48-step window).
The neural baselines (LSTM, GRU, MLP) cluster at 15.73--16.03 hours MAE, indicating that the rich input feature set---particularly the distance-to-destination feature, which is highly predictive---reduces the advantage of sequential modeling.
Ridge regression, despite its simplicity, confirms the value of nonlinear modeling with its substantially higher MAE of 58.30 hours.

The Informer model achieves an MAE of 16.20 hours, which is modestly higher than the baselines trained with symmetric loss.
This difference is attributable to the asymmetric loss function: by penalizing underestimation by a factor of $\alpha = 1.5$, the model systematically shifts predictions upward, which increases symmetric metrics (MAE, MAPE) but reduces the frequency of operationally costly late-arrival predictions.

\subsubsection{Operational Reliability}

In port logistics, underestimating the arrival time is typically more costly than overestimating it, as it can lead to missed berth windows, idle cranes, and cascading schedule disruptions.
The asymmetric loss trades approximately 1 hour of MAE for substantially reduced underestimation rates, making it preferable in operational settings despite its higher symmetric error.

\subsection{Error Analysis}

To understand prediction failures, we analyze the 1,414 test samples (0.05\% of the test set) where the absolute error exceeds 200 hours.
These ``bad cases'' predominantly involve:
(1) extremely long-haul voyages ($>500$ hours) where small relative errors translate to large absolute errors;
(2) voyages with anomalous speed profiles (e.g., prolonged slow steaming or mid-ocean stops);
and (3) voyages with missing or zero-valued weather features.

Fig.~\ref{fig:analysis} visualizes the prediction quality across multiple dimensions.
The scatter plot of predicted vs.\ actual hours shows tight clustering along the diagonal for short and medium voyages, with increased variance for voyages exceeding 400 hours.
The MAE-by-sailing-day plot reveals that prediction accuracy is highest during the first few days of a voyage and degrades for predictions made very early (when more uncertainty remains).

\section{Conclusion}
%==============================================================

We presented a weather-aware vessel ETA prediction system based on a decoupled dual-model architecture.
By separating the sailing-time and port-dwell-time estimation tasks and leveraging the Informer's ProbSparse self-attention for efficient long-sequence processing, our method achieves a test MAE of 16.20 hours on trans-Pacific voyages.
An asymmetric Huber loss is introduced to prioritize operational reliability by reducing underestimation, at a modest cost to symmetric error metrics.

The incorporation of five meteorological features yields a 7.1\% relative improvement over navigation-only inputs (17.43 $\to$ 16.20 hours MAE).
Extensive comparisons against five baselines demonstrate that rich feature engineering---particularly distance-to-destination and weather variables---is a stronger determinant of prediction accuracy than model architecture for this task.

Future work will explore ocean current data integration, multi-task learning for joint sailing and port prediction, and extending the approach to global shipping routes beyond the trans-Pacific corridor.
